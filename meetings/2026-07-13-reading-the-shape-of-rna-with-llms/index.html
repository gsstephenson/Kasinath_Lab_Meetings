The whole project answers one question: given just the letters, can we predict which letters pair up — i.e., the shape?

The picture on the right is the object at the heart of everything: a contact map. Teal dots on the diagonal = letters that pair with a close-by neighbour (easy). The problem

RNA is a string of letters
that folds onto itself

  • An RNA is a chain of four letters — A · U · G · C.
  • The letters pair up by a simple rule: A–U and G–C. Those pairs pin the chain into loops and stems.
  • Which letters pair = the secondary structure. It's the molecule's folded shape.
  • The shape decides the function — same letters, wrong fold, wrong job. Like origami: one flat sheet becomes a crane or a boat depending only on the folds.
seqG G G A A A C C C

( ) = a pair  ·  . = unpaired

Scientists write the fold as matching brackets — a "dot-bracket" string. Our job: predict this from the top row alone.

Goal of this slide: make sure the least-technical person in the room understands "base pairing" and "secondary structure."

Analogy to say out loud: "Think of a shoelace with little magnets along it. Left alone it collapses into a shape as the magnets click together. The letters are the magnets; A clicks to U, G clicks to C. Knowing which magnets clicked tells you the shape."

Point at the bracket example: "Three G's at the start pair with three C's at the end — that's the three brackets closing. The A's in the middle are a loop — the dots. This little hairpin is the simplest fold there is."

Why care (one line): the fold controls whether the RNA switches a gene on, builds a protein, or is a drug target. Predicting it fast, from sequence, is genuinely useful.

The big idea

Stand on the shoulders
of a large RNA model

Instead of building a huge model from scratch, we borrow one that already "knows" RNA — and train only a tiny piece.

  • The model (frozen): DGRNA — a ~100-million-parameter model that has read enormous amounts of RNA. We never change it. 🔒
  • The assistant (trained): a small "probe head" we train to turn the model's knowledge into a map of which letters pair.
  • Freezing the model is cheap, fast, and honest — it measures what the model already understands, not how big an add-on we can train.

Core message: this is "transfer learning" / a "probe" — but say it in plain words.

Analogy: "Imagine hiring an expert who has read every RNA ever written. You don't retrain the expert — you just teach a small intern to ask the expert the one question you care about: which letters pair? The expert is frozen; only the intern learns."

Emphasize the parameter gap on the right: we train ~0.4 million knobs on top of 100 million frozen ones. That's why it's fast and cheap.

"Both directions" matters: a base pair depends on letters that come before and after. We actually tested one-direction models — they were far worse. Reading both ways is essential for pairing. (Backbone bake-off: DGRNA 0.74 vs one-direction models ~0.39–0.44.)

This is the centerpiece — walk it slowly, left to right.

  • Sequence in. Just the letters.
  • Frozen model. Turns each letter into a rich list of numbers ("embedding") that captures its context.
  • Every-vs-every grid. To ask "does letter 5 pair with letter 40?" we pair up all letters into an L×L grid — one cell per possible pair.
  • Probe head (the only trained part). Scores each cell 0–1. The length router is a practical trick: short RNAs use a general head, longer ones use a special "tiled" head (next slide).
  • Contact map. The grid of scores — bright = likely pair.
  • Nested decoder. Turns the fuzzy map into a final, clean list of pairs using a no-crossing rule (slide after next).

Land it: "Two ideas make this better than the rest — how we build the map, and how we decode it. Those are the next two slides."

Innovation 1

Seeing the long-range pairs

  • Old tools only looked near the diagonal — pairs between nearby letters. Fast, but they never even computed the far-apart pairs.
  • Big RNAs (like ribosomes) are full of pairs hundreds of letters apart — the amber dots. Old head scored them at 0%.
  • Our tiled head computes the whole grid in overlapping tiles — so off-diagonal pairs get produced, not dropped.
  • Result: we recover 84% of long-range pairs — with no cost to the easy local ones.
0% → 84%
long-range recall (200–1000 nt)
0.94
local pairs — unchanged
diagonal = old view recovered far pairs

The single biggest win — especially for long & extra-long RNAs.

Analogy: "Imagine proof-reading a book but you're only allowed to compare each word with the words right next to it. You'd catch local typos but miss any rule that connects page 1 to page 300. The old method only read near the diagonal. Our tiled head reads the whole page grid."

Why 'tiled': the full grid is too big to compute at once for a 2,000-letter RNA, so we compute it in overlapping tiles and stitch them — and a normalization trick makes the tiled result numerically identical to doing it all at once.

Hammer the number: from 0% to 84% of long-range pairs, and local pairs actually went up slightly (0.88 → 0.94). No trade-off.

Point at the amber dots animating in: "That's the capability, live — those pairs were invisible before."

Innovation 2

A decoder that can't
draw the impossible

  • The map is fuzzy — the model often over-guesses, proposing pairs that physically cross each other.
  • We checked our data: the true structures have zero crossing pairs. So any crossing guess is guaranteed wrong.
  • Our nested decoder finds the best set of pairs that never cross — throwing out the impossible ones automatically.
  • Free accuracy: the hard medium RNAs jumped 0.63 → 0.72, no family labels, no extra training.

✓ nested — allowed

Brackets may nest inside each other, but they may never cross. The decoder enforces exactly that.

Message: a smart, free fix that fixes over-prediction.

Analogy: "If you know shoelaces in this shoe can never cross, then the moment a guess has two laces crossing, you can throw it out — no biology needed, it's just impossible." That's what the nested decoder does.

Honesty point (professors like this): our benchmark's labels happen to contain no pseudoknots (no crossings) — we verified this across 1.1 million pairs. So on this data, forbidding crossings is exactly right. In the real world some RNAs do cross (pseudoknots); handling those is future work and needs different labels. Be upfront: this decoder is the opposite of a pseudoknot decoder.

Number to land: medium tier +0.088 (0.63 → 0.72) for free, and it helped precision the most (fewer wrong guesses).

Reading the results honestly

Why is medium our lowest tier?

Medium scores 0.72 — below our other tiers (0.90+). But it isn't a weakness of the method: medium is simply where all the hardest molecules live — and we're still #1 on it.

  • It has no easy molecules. The crowd-pleasers (tRNA, 5S) are all short. Medium is made almost entirely of the hard families — introns, tmRNA, SRP, RNaseP.
  • So it's a hard-families problem, not a length problem. These same molecules score low for every tool (tmRNA is ~0.2–0.3 across the board).
  • Part of the gap is a real representation limit: for SRP & tmRNA the frozen model can't "see" ~⅓ of the true pairs — no decoder can recover those.
  • We've already lifted medium a lot — 0.49 → 0.72 — with the tiled head and the no-crossing decoder.

First reassure: "Medium is our lowest tier at 0.72 — but look back at the scoreboard: we're still first on medium, ahead of the next-best tool by about 0.13. It's not that we're bad at medium; it's that medium is hard for everyone."

The key idea, in plain words: "The 'short' tier looks amazing partly because it's full of easy molecules — tRNA, 5S. Those don't exist in medium. Point at the bars: the amber ones (tmRNA 0.36, SRP 0.53, group-I 0.58) are the hard families dragging the average down; 16S in teal (0.90) shows we do great when the molecule is tractable.

Close by handing off to the next slide: "And part of what's left is a genuine limit — the frozen model literally can't see about a third of SRP and tmRNA's pairs. That's the exact problem our fine-tuning experiment went after — which is the next slide."

How we got here

The path — including a dead end

1

Pick the right model

bake-off → a bidirectional (two-way) reader beat one-way models by a mile. Froze DGRNA, trained a small probe head on top.

2

Fix the long-range blind spot

0% → 84% → the tiled head recovered far-apart pairs that older heads never computed. Biggest single jump, especially long/xl.

3

Free wins from smarter decoding

medium 0.63 → 0.72 → the no-crossing decoder + per-family cutoffs cleaned up over-prediction. No new training.

4

Diagnose the last hard families

SRP · RNaseP → a probe showed the frozen model literally can't see ~⅓ of their pairs — a limit no decoder can fix.

✕

Try a targeted fine-tune (LoRA) — and honestly reject it

looked like +0.21 → but our pre-registered honesty test showed it was memorizing look-alikes, not learning new folds. On a truly-new molecule it didn't improve at all. Parked it.

This is the "scientific process" slide — the part a professor grades hardest. Tell it as a story.

  • Step 1: we didn't guess the backbone — we ran a fair bake-off. Two-way reading won.
  • Step 2: found a structural blind spot (long-range pairs) and engineered around it. This is where most of the win came from.
  • Step 3: two "free" ideas — no-crossing decode + per-family thresholds — that needed no extra training.
  • Step 4: we diagnosed why two families still lag: the frozen model can't represent about a third of their pairs. Honest root-cause.
  • Step 5 (the important one): we tried to fix that with a small fine-tune. On paper it looked great — +0.21. But we had written down the test in advance to catch self-deception, and it revealed the gain was mostly memorizing molecules similar to training data. On a genuinely new molecule: no improvement. So we parked it.

Land the lesson: "Real science means building the test that can prove you wrong — and then believing it. A tempting +0.21 that doesn't generalize is not a result."

Honest limits

What we don't claim

  • Two families still lag — SRP and RNaseP. It's a representation limit (the frozen model can't see some pairs), not a decoding bug. Fixing it needs new data we don't have.
  • Nested pairs only. Our labels contain no crossing pairs (pseudoknots), so we measure nested-structure accuracy. Real pseudoknots are future work.
  • Absolute scores are optimistic — this dataset is clean and regular. Trust the head-to-head ranking, not the raw decimals.
  • The LoRA lesson: more trainable capacity ≠ real learning when there's almost no truly-new data to learn from or test on.

Showing limits builds credibility — don't rush this.

SRP / RNaseP: be specific — a diagnostic showed roughly a third of their true pairs get a near-zero score from the frozen backbone. No threshold or decoder can recover a pair the model can't see. The real fix is unfreezing with genuinely novel training data — which barely exists for these families.

Nested-only: our datasets flattened out pseudoknots when they were made. So we're honest that we measure nested accuracy; pseudoknots would need different labels.

The optimism caveat repeats slide 7 on purpose — examiners respect a team that keeps flagging it.

Tie it back: the LoRA dead-end is a finding — it tells us the bottleneck is data, not model size.
