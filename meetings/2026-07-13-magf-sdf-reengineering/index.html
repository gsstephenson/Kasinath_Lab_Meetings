MAGF → MAGC

A year on a membrane-aware decoder, the honest scorecard, and why the failure points straight at signed distance — and at 3D.

We built a decoder meant to keep organelles inside the cell. The mechanism didn't fire — but how it failed names exactly what to build next: not Feedback, but Containment.

George StephensonMembrane2Former2D two-stage MAGF · final results
The problem

A segmentation model forgets that a cell has an inside.

A 3D U-Net scores every voxel independently. Nothing in the loss knows a ribosome can't sit in the extracellular space, or that a cristae must live inside its own mitochondrial membrane. So models emit morphologically illegal maps — organelles floating outside the plasma membrane, lumens with no wall.

That isn't cosmetic. Every downstream measurement we care about — membrane contact sites, surface-to-volume ratios, who-encloses-whom — is a topological question. If the topology is wrong, the biology is wrong.

So we spent the year building a decoder that wouldn't make those mistakes. Here's what it became.

The diagnosis

The model doesn't need a hint. It needs a sign.

Two results, one conclusion. The inert bias says: a suggestion is ignorable — make the geometry load-bearing. The oracle gate says: if you know inside-vs-outside at every point, the leak is impossible. "Inside/outside at every point, carried by a sign" is not a vibe — it's a specific mathematical object.

A soft bias is a suggestion.
A signed distance field's sign is a law.

SDF, in ninety seconds

A signed distance field knows inside from outside — everywhere, not just on the wall.

For every point, store the distance to the nearest membrane, with a sign: negative inside, positive outside, zero on the membrane (the gold line). That one sign bit is a containment license — an organelle is only allowed where the field is negative.

Hover the field to probe the signed distance. Then flip the toggle: watch a leaked organelle get licensed away by the sign — no threshold, no cleanup, just geometry.

Prior work, and our angle

Everyone's using the field. No one's using the sign.

The signed-distance idea is well-trodden in segmentation — and just landed in EM. But every precedent treats the field as a shape / smoothness prior, never as a containment gate:

Xue et al. · AAAI 2020regress a signed distance map as a shape-smoothness target for single organs
COSEM / CellMap · Nature 2021per-class signed distance as a boundary readout, thresholded to a mask

Our angle: we borrow the machinery that makes a learned field trustworthy — an Eikonal term (so the field is a true distance function and its zero-crossing lands on the bilayer) and dual-task SDF↔mask consistency (so containment holds even in unlabeled regions) — and we point it at the sign, as a licensed containment gate across a whole ~48-class organelle hierarchy. A dedicated prior-art search this month confirmed it: that exact intersection — SDF-sign as a licensed, multi-compartment containment gate — is genuinely unclaimed.

So MAGF grows up — from Feedback to Containment.

MAGF → MAGC

Membrane-Aware Geometry Feedback becomes Geometric Containment.

The rename is the fix. Replace the soft, ignorable bias with a learned membrane SDF head whose sign gates the organelle field. The geometry stops being advice and becomes structure — strict where biology is strict, permissive where it isn't:

strict Closed bags

Mitochondria, nucleus, vesicles, lysosomes, peroxisomes — sealed compartments. Lumen strictly inside membrane; organelle strictly inside the cell. The sign-gate is hard here.

permissive Sheets & licensed holes

ER and Golgi cisternae are open sheets — no closed inside. Nuclear pores are real holes; membrane-contact sites are real touches. The gate must allow these, or it's biologically wrong.

Why this makes the biology better. A model that respects topology can't hallucinate an organelle in the ECS or a lumen with no wall — so the maps stay legal, and the measurements built on them (contact sites, volumes, mesh extractions) become trustworthy. And because it licenses the real exceptions instead of erasing them, it's accurate where a naive "strict-everywhere" model would delete a genuine nuclear pore.

But there's a dimension we've been quietly ignoring — literally.

The dimension problem

Containment is a 3D fact. Our model has been reasoning in 2D.

Our decoder is 2.5D — 896² tiles with a few context slices. But "inside" is a 3D enclosure: a membrane has to close over in z to trap a lumen, and a leak that looks sealed on one slice is exposed on the next. A per-slice model can't verify 3D enclosure — so the sign it needed to learn (flip at the bilayer, in 3D) is exactly the thing 2D hides. That's a strong candidate for why the model never found the sign on its own.

And we tried to measure it. We built an oracle that carves "inside this membrane" out of ground truth by closing each membrane's contour, one 2D slice at a time — and it degenerated. Even the plasma membrane, the simplest boundary, fails to close in ~87% of slices, so a contour-based "inside" is undefined there. Tellingly, the one 2D inside/outside that did work — the oracle sign-gate two slides back — only worked because it reads "inside" off the annotated extracellular space as a complement, never by closing a contour; interior compartments have no such external label, so they're left with the contour construction that degenerates. (Nuclear pores we couldn't test at all — none were sampled in the pooled crops.)

Honest read: a measurement limit — not proof the licensing holds, and not by itself proof that 2D is too little (a non-contour interior, or a wider field of view, is the cleaner next check). The load-bearing reason to go 3D is the one above: containment has to close in z. That's what the 2.5D retrain settles.

The plan: 2D features, 3D geometry. Don't retrain a native 3D backbone — expensive, and it throws away DINOv3's pretraining. Cache the frozen DINOv3 features, then run a (2+1)D decoder over a z-window (2D spatial + 1D depth convolutions) that predicts a 3D membrane SDF. The sign-gate enforces true closed surfaces in 3D — and the same field completes the cryo missing wedge. This is the flagship's capstone.

DINOv3 stays. The decoder grows a dimension.

The two missing pieces

Everything upstream is optimized. Two moves close the gap.

The foundation — DINOv3, the two-stage decoder, the corrected readout — is tuned and working. The delta to state of the art isn't a new architecture. It's two things the current model provably lacks:

①

The sign

a learned membrane SDF — MAGC

Replace the ignorable soft bias with a signed-distance head whose sign licenses containment. Geometry stops being a suggestion and becomes a law — violations go to zero by construction.

②

The dimension

pseudo-3D on cached DINOv3

Lift the per-slice features into a (2+1)D decoder so the sign-gate enforces true enclosure in 3D — where "inside" is actually defined, and where the sign finally has a bilayer to flip at.

Bolt both onto the optimized base — and this reaches its state-of-the-art form.

Where this goes

From a scaffold that leaks to a model that can't.

The signed model we have is the baseline the flagship beats — its late leak is precisely the problem MAGC removes. The build order:

  1. MAGC kernel · 2.5D membrane sign-gate.On the existing DINOv3 forward: a trained sign head supervised by signed distance to the membrane; child occupancy = own-sign ∧ parent-interior-sign at the readout. The smallest real piece of the flagship.
  2. Licensed geometry — the intellectual heart.The strict / permissive / licensed table across the containment DAG: bags strict, sheets open, pores & contacts licensed. Where "biologically accurate" is won.
  3. Cross-modal cryo — the Nature Methods leg.The containment prior transfers to cryo-ET with composite-only labels: invariant biology, a new modality.
  4. 3D flagship — the capstone.Cached DINOv3 features → (2+1)D decoder over a z-window → 3D SDF: true closed surfaces and full missing-wedge completion. The "true biological decoder."

Running now: an out-of-the-box nnU-Net baseline, fully verified, launching this week. The headline isn't Dice — it's topological validity: our readout produces zero containment violations where a voxel model structurally cannot.
